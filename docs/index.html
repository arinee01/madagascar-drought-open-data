1. Introduction

Purpose & public interest

Drought in Madagascar is not a distant headline; it is a recurring, place-specific reality that reshapes harvests, water access, and local resilience. Yet the public story is often fragmented—numbers scattered across reports, maps published without context, and indicators hard to compare across time and regions.

This project offers a clear, citizen‑friendly entry point into that complexity. It brings together trusted open datasets, aligns them by region, and translates them into visual, readable outputs that help people understand where drought pressures are concentrated and how they evolve. The goal is not prediction or alarmism, but transparent, reproducible insight.

The reuse hypothesis behind this project is that heterogeneous open datasets—when aligned at a common regional scale and documented with transparent metadata—can support fair and bias-aware interpretations of climate vulnerability without relying on personal or sensitive data.

The unit of analysis is the administrative region (ADM1), chosen to balance spatial detail with data availability and to avoid misleading comparisons at overly granular levels.

The project does not aim to provide forecasts, early-warning systems, or causal explanations. Instead, it focuses on descriptive transparency: making existing indicators comparable, traceable, and accessible without overstating their precision or policy implications.

From the start, the work is grounded in open data ethics: clear licensing, documented provenance, and careful aggregation that avoids individual-level inference. The result is a small but rigorous public resource—part narrative, part dashboard—designed for students, journalists, and citizens who want to explore climate vulnerability through evidence they can trace, question, and reuse over time.

2. Scenario

Research question & scope

Madagascar is one of the countries most exposed to the impacts of climate variability, with recurrent droughts affecting large parts of its territory. These phenomena have direct consequences on food security, agricultural productivity, access to water, and the resilience of local communities.

In recent years, drought episodes in southern and central regions of Madagascar have attracted international attention, often described as humanitarian emergencies. However, public understanding of these events is frequently fragmented, relying on isolated indicators or media narratives that lack territorial and temporal context.

This project is grounded in the idea that open data can play a crucial role in improving transparency and public awareness around climate-related phenomena. By reusing and integrating heterogeneous open datasets, it becomes possible to move beyond anecdotal descriptions and provide a structured, data-driven overview of drought conditions at the regional level.

Reuse hypothesis

The reuse hypothesis underlying this project is that combining climate indicators with territorial boundaries through a mash-up of open datasets enables a more meaningful interpretation of drought patterns than the use of single datasets in isolation.

In particular, spatial aggregation at the regional level allows users to explore how drought-related indicators vary across the territory, supporting comparative analysis while avoiding individual-level or sensitive data.

Public interest and target audience

The project is designed for a broad audience, including citizens, journalists, researchers, and public institutions interested in climate vulnerability and environmental sustainability.

The results are presented through human-readable visualizations and are published using open standards to ensure accessibility and reuse. No prior technical knowledge is required to explore the visual outputs, while machine-readable metadata supports integration into open data catalogs and further analytical reuse.

Ethical framing and limitations

The project does not aim to predict future drought events, assess individual risk, or support operational decision-making. Instead, it provides an exploratory and descriptive view of aggregated indicators derived from open data sources.

By focusing exclusively on aggregated regional data, the project avoids the use of personal or sensitive information and mitigates the risk of misinterpretation, stigmatization, or discriminatory use of the data.

All interpretations should be considered within the limits of the underlying datasets, their update frequency, and their original purposes. The visualizations are intended to support awareness and understanding, not to replace domain-specific analyses.

What this project does not do
  • No individual-level data or profiling.
  • No causal claims (correlation != causation).
  • No ranking of "worst communities" without context.

4. Quality analysis of the datasets

Completeness / consistency / timeliness

In line with the principles of open data reuse promoted by the Copernicus Programme, the World Meteorological Organization, and the World Bank, a comprehensive data quality assessment was conducted for all datasets used in this project. The objective of this evaluation is to ensure that the regional analysis of drought risk and environmental stress in Madagascar is based on reliable, interoperable, and ethically reusable open data.

The assessment follows four core data quality dimensions: accuracy, coherence, completeness, and timeliness.

1. Accuracy (syntactic and semantic)

Monthly precipitation data (GPCC)

Precipitation data is derived from the Global Precipitation Climatology Centre (GPCC), which aggregates rain gauge observations from multiple international meteorological networks. The dataset is considered semantically accurate, as it represents direct physical measurements of rainfall. Minor uncertainties may arise in regions with sparse ground stations, particularly in remote or rural areas of Madagascar.

Standardized Precipitation Index (SPI)

The SPI dataset is calculated using precipitation time series and standardized statistical methods. It provides a well-established indicator for drought conditions. While the index is methodologically robust, its accuracy depends on the quality and spatial coverage of the underlying precipitation data.

Risk of drought impact for agriculture

The drought risk indicator is derived from model-based classifications combining multiple climate inputs. Although it does not represent direct observations, it offers a consistent and interpretable assessment of agricultural drought risk. The categorical nature of the data (low, medium, high) reduces numerical precision but improves semantic clarity for decision-making contexts.

2. Coherence

All datasets were harmonized at the regional level, using consistent region names and temporal references. Monthly observations were aggregated to annual averages for 2024 and 2025, ensuring temporal alignment across indicators.

The relationship between precipitation, SPI values, and drought risk indicators exhibited coherent patterns. Regions with persistent negative SPI values generally corresponded to higher proportions of medium or high agricultural drought risk, which aligns with established climatological expectations.

No conflicting spatial or temporal identifiers were observed after preprocessing, indicating a high level of interoperability among the datasets.

3. Completeness

Precipitation (GPCC)

The dataset provides extensive monthly coverage; however, some regions and months contain missing values, reflecting gaps in meteorological station reporting. Annual averages were computed using available months only, without imputing missing values.

SPI dataset

SPI data follows the same temporal structure as the precipitation dataset and shares similar completeness limitations. Missing values were preserved as null entries to avoid artificial data generation.

Agricultural drought risk

The drought risk dataset covers most regions and months but contains occasional gaps due to model constraints or insufficient input data. These gaps were explicitly retained and documented.

Some incompleteness is therefore present across datasets, but it is handled transparently. No synthetic or interpolated values were introduced, in accordance with ethical data reuse principles.

4. Timeliness

The datasets include observations up to 2025, making them suitable for recent drought analysis. However, slight delays between observation, processing, and publication are inherent to climate and satellite-based datasets.

The precipitation and SPI indicators are updated regularly, while drought risk classifications may lag slightly due to model recalibration cycles. Despite these delays, the datasets remain sufficiently timely for regional-scale drought assessment and comparative analysis between 2024 and 2025.

5. Legal analysis

Ownership / licenses / privacy

This legal assessment is grounded in international open data principles and the national legal framework applicable to Madagascar, as well as the reuse policies of major international data providers such as the World Bank, FAO, Copernicus, and the Global Drought Observatory. The analysis follows the principles of lawful reuse, transparency, and ethical compliance as required in open access research projects.

1. Privacy issues

All datasets used in this project are classified as non-personal data. None of the sources include information that can directly or indirectly identify individuals, such as names, personal identifiers, addresses, biometric data, or geolocation data linked to individuals.

Datasets consist exclusively of:

  • Aggregated environmental indicators (e.g., soil moisture anomalies, precipitation anomalies, drought indicators)
  • Water stress and water withdrawal indicators at national or regional level
  • Agricultural and land-use statistics expressed as percentages or indices
  • Administrative and geographic boundaries at the regional level

Even when socio-environmental pressure indicators are used (e.g., water stress or agricultural land share), all values are aggregated at regional or national scale, eliminating any risk of re-identification or deanonymization. Therefore, the project does not fall under the scope of personal data protection regulations such as GDPR or comparable privacy laws.

2. Intellectual property rights

All datasets originate from official public institutions or international organizations that explicitly promote open data reuse. The main data providers include:

  • World Bank Open Data - water stress, agricultural land, water withdrawal indicators
  • FAO (Food and Agriculture Organization) - agricultural and environmental statistics
  • Copernicus Programme / Global Drought Observatory (JRC) - drought, vegetation, and soil moisture indicators
  • Humanitarian OpenStreetMap Team (HOT) - geospatial data on waterways and infrastructure
  • Administrative boundary datasets sourced from international open geospatial repositories

These organizations retain authorship but allow reuse, transformation, and redistribution of the data under open or permissive licensing conditions, provided that proper attribution is given.

3. Licensing and reuse

The datasets used in this project are distributed under open licenses compatible with reuse, including:

  • CC BY 4.0 (World Bank, FAO datasets)
  • Copernicus Open Access Licence (environmental and satellite-derived indicators)
  • CC BY 4.0 (geoBoundaries, GPCC)
  • Open Database License (ODbL) or equivalent (OpenStreetMap / HOT data)

To ensure full compatibility and openness, the final mashup dataset produced in this project is released under the Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 (CC BY 4.0) license. This allows free reuse, redistribution, and adaptation of the dataset for both academic and non-commercial purposes, provided that attribution to the original sources is maintained.

4. Access limitations

All datasets are freely accessible online, without registration, subscription fees, or access restrictions. No dataset includes classified, military, or security-sensitive information.

The data complies with open access requirements:

  • Machine-readable formats (CSV, XLSX, GeoJSON)
  • Public documentation and metadata
  • No technical or legal barriers to access

Thus, no additional legal constraints apply to the use or publication of the integrated datasets.

5. Economic conditions

All data sources were accessed free of charge. No licensing fees, royalties, or commercial restrictions apply. The reuse of data respects the original platforms' terms of service and does not involve resale or proprietary exploitation.

The project aligns with open data economic principles by:

  • Encouraging reuse without financial barriers
  • Supporting downstream innovation and analysis
  • Avoiding vendor lock-in or platform dependency

6. Temporal aspects

The datasets used in the mashup differ in update frequency and temporal coverage:

  • World Bank indicators: updated annually; some indicators have a reporting delay of 1-2 years
  • FAO datasets: updated annually or biannually
  • Copernicus / Global Drought Observatory: near-real-time or monthly updates, depending on the indicator
  • Geospatial boundary data: relatively stable, updated only when administrative changes occur

All temporal limitations are explicitly documented to avoid misinterpretation and ensure transparency in longitudinal analysis.

7. Final note on publication and compliance

This project follows best practices in open data reuse by clearly attributing all original data sources, documenting all transformations, aggregations, and assumptions, preserving transparency in methodological choices, and publishing outputs under an open and interoperable license.

The legal framework of this project aligns with international standards, including:

  • The International Open Data Charter
  • UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), particularly SDG 6 (Clean Water) and SDG 13 (Climate Action)
  • UNESCO Open Science Recommendation
  • EU Copernicus Open Data Policy

As a result, the legal reuse of data in this project is compliant, transparent, and consistent with both national and international open access principles.

The project is developed in accordance with international open data principles and best practices for transparency, attribution, and responsible reuse.

6. Ethics analysis

Fairness / bias / do-no-harm

The ethical analysis of this project follows principles outlined in EU Data Ethics Guidelines, the Open Data Institute's Ethics Canvas, and international recommendations on responsible reuse of environmental and climate data. Given the project's focus on drought, water stress, and agricultural vulnerability in Madagascar, particular attention was paid to ethical considerations related to data selection, aggregation, interpretation, and communication.

1. Data ethics principles

Human-centric and societal relevance

The primary objective of this project is to improve understanding of drought-related risks and environmental stress at the regional level in Madagascar, with a particular focus on agriculture and water availability. The analysis is designed to support evidence-based decision-making and resilience-building strategies, especially in regions that are structurally vulnerable due to climatic variability, limited infrastructure, and economic dependence on rain-fed agriculture. The project does not seek to rank or judge regions, but to highlight patterns that may inform adaptive and preventive policies.

Fairness and equity

All indicators are aggregated at the regional level, ensuring that no individual or household-level data is used. The analysis avoids framing regions with higher drought risk or lower precipitation as failures and instead emphasizes systemic climatic and environmental factors. Differences between regions are interpreted in light of geographic, climatic, and ecological conditions rather than governance or social performance.

Transparency and reproducibility

All datasets used in the project originate from publicly accessible open data sources, such as international climate and environmental data providers. Data transformations, including temporal aggregation from monthly to annual values and the construction of derived indicators, are fully documented. No hidden assumptions, undocumented filtering, or opaque modeling steps were applied, ensuring reproducibility and auditability of the analysis.

Accountability in data reuse

The project explicitly respects the original scope and limitations of each dataset. Indicators were only used at the spatial and temporal resolution supported by the data providers. When region-level tabular data was not available (e.g., for certain satellite-derived indicators), these datasets were excluded from the quantitative mashup and used only for qualitative interpretation. This decision reflects a deliberate ethical choice to avoid artificial spatial disaggregation or synthetic data generation.

Privacy and respect for affected populations

No personal data, microdata, or sensitive individual-level information was used. Environmental and agricultural indicators are expressed as indices, averages, or proportions at the regional level. This approach eliminates any risk of re-identification and ensures respectful representation of populations affected by drought and water stress. The framing of results focuses on risk reduction and preparedness rather than attributing responsibility to specific communities.

2. Ethical concerns and mitigation measures

Avoiding data fabrication and false precision

A key ethical challenge in climate and environmental analysis is the temptation to infer regional values from national-level data or to visually approximate numerical values from maps. This project explicitly avoided such practices. Indicators without reliable region-level tabular data were excluded from quantitative modeling, preventing the introduction of unverifiable or misleading information.

Handling data gaps and uncertainty

Missing values and incomplete temporal coverage were preserved as such, rather than being filled through interpolation or estimation. This choice prioritizes transparency over completeness and ensures that uncertainty remains visible to users of the dataset. All limitations related to data coverage are clearly documented.

Responsible interpretation of results

The analysis does not claim causal relationships between drought indicators and agricultural outcomes. Correlations between precipitation, SPI, and drought risk are presented as signals of environmental stress, not as deterministic explanations. Visualizations and summaries are explicitly framed as descriptive and exploratory.

Geographical sensitivity

Given Madagascar's regional diversity and socio-economic disparities, particular care was taken to avoid reinforcing stereotypes or stigmatization. Regions experiencing higher drought risk are contextualized within broader climatic patterns rather than portrayed as anomalous or deficient.

Public communication and data literacy

The project is designed to be accessible to a broad audience, including students, researchers, and policy-oriented stakeholders. Simplified indicators and clear explanations are provided alongside technical documentation to support informed interpretation without oversimplification.

3. Final ethical considerations

The ethical safeguards adopted in this project reflect international best practices in open data reuse and climate data governance. By prioritizing transparency, avoiding synthetic data generation, and respecting both environmental and social sensitivities, the project demonstrates responsible and ethical use of open environmental data.

The approach aligns with global ethical frameworks promoting fairness, accountability, and sustainability, and supports the use of open data as a tool for understanding climate risks and supporting resilient development in vulnerable contexts such as Madagascar.

7. Technical analysis (formats, metadata, URI, provenance)

Formats / metadata / URI / provenance

This technical analysis describes the formats, metadata model, identification strategy, and provenance mechanisms adopted in the project to support transparency, interoperability, and reuse in accordance with the EU Open Data Guidelines.

The project is designed to produce reusable and interoperable outputs by combining open data formats with semantic web technologies and standard metadata vocabularies.

Formats

Original datasets are provided in open, machine-readable formats, including CSV for statistical indicators and GeoJSON for administrative boundaries. These formats are widely supported and suitable for both analysis and public dissemination.

The data processing workflow produces aggregated mash-up datasets that are published as CSV for tabular reuse and as GeoJSON to support spatial visualization. In addition, dataset metadata is published in RDF/Turtle format to enable Linked Open Data reuse and semantic interoperability.

Formats summary:

  • Inputs: CSV (statistical indicators), GeoJSON (administrative boundaries)
  • Outputs: CSV (mash-up dataset, SPI time series), GeoJSON (regions for mapping), RDF/Turtle (metadata)

Metadata and Linked Open Data

Dataset metadata is modeled using the DCAT-AP application profile, which is the reference standard for describing open datasets in the European context. DCAT-AP ensures interoperability with European open data portals and supports automated discovery and reuse.

The metadata describes both the original datasets and the mash-up output dataset, including titles, descriptions, publishers, licenses, spatial coverage, and available distributions. Licenses are expressed as resolvable URIs to ensure legal clarity and machine-readability.

URI strategy

The project adopts a stable base URI rooted in the GitHub Pages domain: https://arinee01.github.io/madagascar-drought-open-data/.

Specific resources are identified as follows:

  • Catalog: https://arinee01.github.io/madagascar-drought-open-data/rdf/catalog.ttl
  • Datasets metadata: https://arinee01.github.io/madagascar-drought-open-data/rdf/datasets.ttl
  • Mash-up dataset identifier: https://arinee01.github.io/madagascar-drought-open-data/#dataset-mashup

Vocabularies

The RDF metadata relies exclusively on well-established and widely adopted vocabularies, in order to maximize interoperability and avoid unnecessary complexity.

  • dcat: Description of datasets and distributions
  • dct: Titles, descriptions, licenses, spatial and temporal properties
  • prov: Provenance relations (prov:wasDerivedFrom)

Provenance

The mash-up dataset is explicitly documented as derived from the original source datasets using the PROV-O property prov:wasDerivedFrom. This provenance information makes the data integration process transparent and supports reproducibility of the workflow.

By documenting how output datasets are produced from their sources, the project enables informed reuse and long-term maintenance of the datasets.

8. Sustainability of updates over time

Lifecycle / reproducibility / versioning

Dataset lifecycle, reproducibility, and versioning

The datasets used in this project are published by international and public institutions and are periodically updated according to their respective publication policies. The project is designed to support sustainable reuse by clearly separating the data lifecycle into distinct phases: original datasets (input), curated mash-up datasets (processed), and published outputs (visualizations and Linked Open Data).

The entire workflow is reproducible and documented through open-source code, version control, and structured metadata. Dataset updates can be integrated by re-running the processing pipeline and regenerating the output datasets without modifying the visualization or publication layers.

At the current stage, data curation and aggregation are semi-manual. The repository documents the key normalization steps as scripts in scripts/ (SPI normalization and mashup CSV cleanup), which can be rerun when new data arrives. This supports transparent updates while keeping the workflow lightweight.

Versioning is managed through the GitHub repository, where each update to the datasets, metadata, or RDF assertions is tracked and documented. Changes in the mash-up dataset trigger corresponding updates in the DCAT-AP metadata and RDF serializations, ensuring consistency between data, documentation, and licenses.

The publication architecture—based on open formats (CSV, GeoJSON, RDF), persistent URIs, and GitHub Pages—supports long-term accessibility and reuse by third parties. This design enables future extensions of the project, including the addition of new indicators, temporal dimensions, or updated geographic boundaries, while preserving transparency and reproducibility over time.

As a sustainability measure, any changes in source data, methodology, or assumptions are documented alongside the updated outputs to preserve interpretability for users over time.
